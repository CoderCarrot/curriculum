5 Technologies Powered by Algorithms That You Use Every Day

You probably use technologies that are powered by algorithms every day. Algorithms can add a new level of intelligence to many of the products and services we use.

By: Cullen Lewis

tilr isn’t the only company that uses algorithms to add an unparalleled level of intelligence to the technology we use every single day. Some of the world’s largest companies owe their success to algorithms. They are hidden within almost every service you use daily, from Siri to Spotify. Here are five technologies powered by algorithms that you may use every day.

 

Siri

The world’s most beloved personal assistant has come a long way since its release in 2010. Its highly complex algorithm can organize through an innumerable amount of data to give you the information you need in a matter of seconds. How does it work, you ask? After you give Siri a command or ask it a question, the algorithm puts your voice into text to code it. Siri then dissects the code to pick up on specific keywords and phrases to figure out the context of the command. After the context is decided, the algorithm then decides if the answer can be found within the account’s personal data (contacts, saved photos, music, etc.), or on an online server (trivia, weather, news, etc). Finally, once Siri finds what it thinks you are looking for, it is able to craft a developed and cohesive sentence in return, all within a blink of an eye.

 

 

Google Maps

We would be completely lost without Google Maps in our lives today – literally. The highly popular navigation service is able to create seamless directions thanks to algorithms. You may have been lucky to see a Google street view car roam around your neighborhood at one point. If you are not familiar with these vehicles, these cars have a camera attached to the top of it taking dozens of 360-degree photos within seconds. Once all the data is collected from cities around the world, an algorithm is able to go through billions of photos and extract relevant information such as street names, shops, and house addresses. This, in turn, provides the service with a much more accurate and updated mapping service. So when you need directions to the new restaurant that opened up across town, thank the algorithm that got you there.

 

 

Netflix

Ever wondered how the “Suggestions for You” tab on Netflix knows you so well? The video streaming giants over at Netflix have developed a personalization algorithm that perfectly blends what you want to watch and new suggestions that will turn into one of your favorites. Netflix has also developed a unique algorithm that tackles any streaming service’s most frustrating problem: buffering. Their algorithm is able to determine each video frame and compact it to lowest possible bandwidth without sacrificing video quality. That means less time waiting for your show to buffer and more time spent binge watching!

 

 

Predictive Text

You either love it or hate it, but regardless predictive text or autocorrect has become an integral tool in the way we communicate online. Developed by Google in the 1990’s, this technology uses something called Bayes’ Theorem, which predicts the outcome based on prior knowledge. Therefore, the algorithm is always learning and taking note on the most common misspelling of words.

 

 

 

Spotify

Spotify is without a doubt the world’s most popular music discovery and streaming service, and much of their success comes from their algorithm-based playlist, Discover Weekly. This curated playlist picks songs that you will fall in love with upon the first listen. But how does the algorithm find such awesome music for you, personally? The answer is actually other people’s playlists. In a recent interview with Quartz, Spotify’s Matthew Ogle explains that the Discover Weekly algorithm is based off both your’s and other users’ music tastes. First, the algorithm will look at your most listened to songs and find similar songs in the same genre. Then it will view billions of other user’s playlists to find if you share any similar songs and genres within those playlists as well. As a result, you receive both a personal and eclectic group of songs that could become your next obsession.
